Basic Knowledge on DRRM Approach

Disaster Risk Reduction and Management

Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) focuses on reducing disaster risks and minimizing their impact on communities. Disasters can happen anytime and often occur without warning. Because of this, preparedness, awareness, and education are essential in protecting lives and property.

What is a Disaster?

A disaster is a serious disruption in the functioning of a community that causes widespread human, economic, environmental, or material losses. It occurs when a hazard exceeds the ability of a community to cope using its own resources.

Types of Disasters

Natural Disasters

  • Tropical cyclones
  • Floods
  • Earthquakes
  • Tsunamis
  • Volcanic eruptions
  • Landslides
  • Drought

Man-Made Disasters

  • Fires
  • Terrorist attacks
  • Pollution
  • Civil disturbance
  • Armed conflict

Technological Disasters

  • Aircraft crashes
  • Chemical explosions
  • Nuclear accidents
  • Toxic waste incidents

2004 Indian Ocean Earthquake and Tsunami

On December 26, 2004, a magnitude 9.1–9.3 earthquake struck off the coast of Sumatra, Indonesia near the Sunda megathrust fault, triggering massive tsunami waves reaching heights of up to 30 meters. The disaster affected 14 countries and resulted in approximately 230,000 to 280,000 deaths.

Indonesia was the hardest hit, particularly the Aceh province. The tragedy exposed the lack of early warning systems and significantly increased global awareness regarding disaster preparedness and resilient infrastructure development.

The Power of Education: The Story of Tilly Smith

In 2004, a 10-year-old British girl named Tilly Smith recognized the warning signs of a tsunami while vacationing in Thailand. Having recently learned about tsunamis in school, she warned her family and others at Mai Khao Beach, prompting evacuation before the waves struck.

Her actions demonstrated that knowledge and quick thinking can save lives. Her story emphasizes the importance of disaster education and preparedness programs worldwide.

Philippine Disaster Risk Profile

The Philippines is one of the most disaster-prone countries in the world due to its geographic location. The country lies along the Pacific Typhoon Belt, where many tropical cyclones develop every year.

It is also located along the Pacific Ring of Fire, an area where many earthquakes and volcanic eruptions occur. In addition, the Philippine Fault Zone runs across the archipelago, increasing the risk of earthquakes.

Because of these natural conditions, the Philippines frequently experiences typhoons, floods, earthquakes, landslides, and volcanic eruptions.

Republic Act 10121

Republic Act 10121, also known as the Philippine Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act of 2010, established a comprehensive approach to disaster management in the country.

The law promotes preparedness, prevention, mitigation, and recovery instead of focusing only on disaster response. It also requires the integration of disaster education into school curricula, including the National Service Training Program (NSTP).

Proactive and Reactive Risk Management

Reactive risk management focuses on responding after a disaster occurs, such as rescue operations and relief distribution.

Proactive risk management involves preventive measures taken before disasters occur, including hazard mapping, infrastructure strengthening, and public awareness campaigns.

The DRRM approach integrates both strategies but prioritizes prevention to minimize losses.

The Four Pillars of the DRRM Framework

1. Disaster Prevention and Mitigation

Actions taken before a disaster to reduce or avoid risks and minimize potential impacts.

  • Hazard and risk mapping
  • Flood control systems
  • Forecasting and monitoring
  • Public awareness campaigns

2. Disaster Preparedness

Actions taken before a disaster to ensure readiness and efficient response.

  • Evacuation plans and drills
  • Stockpiling emergency supplies
  • Training volunteers and responders

3. Disaster Response

Actions taken during or immediately after a disaster to protect life and property.

  • Emergency evacuation
  • Search and rescue operations
  • Relief distribution
  • Temporary shelters and medical assistance

4. Disaster Rehabilitation and Recovery

Efforts focused on restoring essential services and rebuilding communities after a disaster.

  • Post-disaster needs assessment
  • Temporary housing for displaced families
  • Restoration of water and electricity services

Applying the DRRM Approach in Everyday Life

Disaster risk reduction is not solely the responsibility of the government. Individuals must stay informed, participate in drills, prepare emergency kits, and assist others during emergencies.

Preparedness empowers individuals to respond confidently during crises and contributes to building disaster-resilient communities.

Earthquake Readiness

What is an Earthquake?

An earthquake is the shaking of the Earth's surface caused by a sudden release of energy within the Earth's crust. Earthquakes can cause severe damage to buildings, infrastructure, and communities.

Types of Earthquakes

Tectonic Earthquakes

Caused by movement of tectonic plates along fault lines.

Volcanic Earthquakes

Associated with volcanic activity and magma movement.

Collapse Earthquakes

Caused by collapse of underground caves or mines.

Explosion Earthquakes

Caused by explosions such as mining or nuclear testing.

Induced Earthquakes

Triggered by human activities like drilling or reservoir construction.

Common Effects of Earthquakes

  • Ground shaking
  • Building collapse
  • Landslides
  • Tsunamis

What to Do During an Earthquake

Duck

  • Drop to your hands and knees.
  • Stay low to avoid being knocked over.

Cover

  • Protect your head and neck.
  • Take cover under a sturdy table or desk.
  • Avoid windows and glass.

Hold

  • Hold on to furniture.
  • Stay in position until shaking stops.

If Outside

  • Move to an open area.
  • Avoid buildings, trees, and power lines.

If Inside a Vehicle

  • Stop safely away from bridges and buildings.
  • Stay inside until shaking stops.

If There Is No Table Nearby

  • Get down on your hands and knees.
  • Cover your head and neck with your arms.
  • Move near an interior wall away from windows and heavy objects.
  • Stay in position until shaking stops.

After the Shaking Stops

Stay Calm

  • Prepare for possible aftershocks.

Check for Injuries

  • Check yourself and others.
  • Provide first aid if needed.

Inspect Surroundings

  • Watch for falling debris or unstable structures.
  • Check for gas leaks, fires, or exposed wires.

Evacuate Safely

  • Use marked evacuation routes.
  • Avoid elevators.
  • Proceed to designated assembly points.

Evacuation Guidelines

  • Stay calm and alert.
  • Follow designated routes.
  • Do not run.
  • Assist those in need.
  • Avoid hazards.
  • Leave belongings behind.
  • Stay with your group.
  • Report to your leader at the assembly point.
  • Wait for further instructions.

What to Do After an Earthquake

  • Remain calm and alert for aftershocks.
  • Check for injuries and assist others.
  • Inspect for structural damage and hazards.
  • Avoid beaches or coastal areas due to possible tsunamis.
  • Follow official announcements and evacuation orders.
  • Help vulnerable individuals such as children and the elderly.

A Tragedy of Unpreparedness: Nevado del Ruiz Volcano (1985)

In November 1985, the Nevado del Ruiz Volcano in Colombia erupted. Although the eruption itself was not massive, it melted the volcano’s ice cap, triggering deadly lahars that destroyed nearby communities.

One of the most tragic victims was Omayra Sánchez Garzón, a 13-year-old girl trapped under debris in Armero. Her story became a global symbol of the consequences of inadequate preparedness and delayed emergency response.

This tragedy highlights the importance of readiness and proactive disaster management. It reminds us that preparedness should never begin after disaster strikes.

Importance of Preparedness

Earthquake preparedness is a shared responsibility. Through proper education, drills, and awareness, communities can reduce panic, minimize injuries, and save lives. The question remains: Are we truly ready when disaster strikes?
